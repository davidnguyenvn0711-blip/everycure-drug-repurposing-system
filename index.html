Open Science Project

DrugBridge

A beginner-friendly tool that discovers new uses for existing medicines by finding shared biological targets between drugs and diseases.

About

What is drug repurposing?

Most medicines take over a decade and billions of dollars to develop from scratch. But here is a smarter shortcut: what if a drug that already exists could treat a completely different disease?

This idea is called drug repurposing -- and it works because many diseases share the same broken biology. If a drug fixes that broken biology for one disease, it might fix it for another too.

DrugBridge is a tiny version of systems like EveryCure, which use data to search for these hidden connections at massive scale. Our version uses a small set of real drugs and diseases so you can see exactly how the logic works.

Methodology

How does it find matches?

Every drug in our system has a list of biological targets -- proteins or pathways in the body that it affects. Every disease also has a list of biological targets that drive it.

The system simply asks: do this drug and this disease share any targets? If yes, that is a candidate match. The more targets they share, the stronger the match.

1
Map the biology

Each drug and disease is described by a list of biological targets -- the proteins and pathways involved.

2
Find the overlap

The system compares every drug against every disease, looking for shared targets using set intersection.

3
Score the match

A Jaccard similarity score (0.0 to 1.0) measures the proportion of overlap. Higher means a stronger connection.

4
Generate a hypothesis

Each match becomes a plain-English explanation that a scientist could investigate further in the lab.

Score = shared targets ÷ all unique targets combined
shared targets Targets that appear on BOTH the drug list and the disease list
all unique targets Every target from either list combined, counting each only once
score of 1.00 Perfect overlap -- the drug hits every target the disease involves
score of 0.20 Weak overlap -- only one target in common out of many

Glossary

Key terms, explained simply

Biological target A protein, gene, or pathway in the body that a drug interacts with. Think of it as the drug's "landing spot" inside your cells.
Drug repurposing Finding a new medical use for a drug that is already approved and proven safe. Faster and cheaper than building a new drug from scratch.
Jaccard score A mathematical way to measure how much two sets overlap. A score of 1.0 means total overlap; 0.0 means nothing in common.
mTOR A protein that acts like a master switch for cell growth. When it misfires, it contributes to cancer, Alzheimer's, PCOS, and more.
Inflammatory cytokines Chemical messengers that trigger inflammation. Useful in small doses; destructive when overproduced, as seen in autoimmune diseases.
Set intersection A Python operation that finds items appearing in two lists at the same time. The core operation behind every match in this system.
Autophagy inhibition When cells cannot clean up their own broken parts. The word "autophagy" means "self-eating." Disrupting it contributes to lupus flares and neurodegeneration.
